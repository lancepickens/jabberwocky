# Key Technical Barriers to Commercial Fusion

## 1. Tritium Supply

- **Price**: $30,000-$35,000 per gram (one of the most expensive materials on Earth)
- **Global production**: <4 kg/year from ~30 CANDU reactors worldwide
- **Half-life**: 12.3 years (decays continuously)
- **Requirement**: A 1 GW fusion plant would consume ~56 kg of tritium per year
- **Breeding ratio needed**: >1.05 (must produce more tritium than consumed)
- **Chicken-and-egg problem**: Testing breeding blankets requires a fusion reactor, but building a reactor requires proven breeding blankets
- **Lithium-6 bottleneck**: Natural lithium is only 7.5% Li-6; breeding blankets need 30-90% enrichment; production concentrated in Russia and China

## 2. Materials Science

- **No known solution at scale**: No mass-manufacturable material can simultaneously withstand:
  - Neutron flux: ~10^14 n/cm^2/s
  - Temperature: 1,100-1,500 K
  - Elemental transmutation over months/years
- **Plasma contamination**: Some materials contaminate plasma, reducing efficiency
- **Tritium retention**: Some materials absorb unreacted tritium, losing precious fuel
- **Tungsten trade-offs**: Best current option but has issues with eddy currents, melting in off-normal events, and plasma contamination when sputtered
- **Development timeline**: Materials qualification historically takes 15-25 years

## 3. Plasma Containment & Control

- **Burning plasma control**: No device has operated with a self-heating burning plasma for extended periods
- **Disruptions**: Tokamak plasmas can suddenly lose confinement, releasing enormous energy to walls
- **Confinement time**: EAST achieved 1,066 seconds H-mode (Jan 2025), but at low Q; high-Q sustained operation is much harder
- **Scaling uncertainty**: Performance at ITER/power-plant scale involves physics not fully validated at smaller scale

## 4. Net Electricity Generation

- **No fusion device has ever generated electricity**
- **Thermal conversion**: Must capture neutron energy in blanket, transfer heat to working fluid, drive turbines
- **Direct conversion**: Helion's approach (direct electricity from changing magnetic fields) is unproven at scale
- **Balance of plant**: Cryogenics, magnets, heating systems, tritium processing all consume significant power
- **Reliability**: Power plants need >90% availability; fusion devices currently operate in experimental pulses

## 5. Regulatory Framework

- **NRC jurisdiction**: US Nuclear Regulatory Commission is developing fusion-specific regulations
- **Timeline uncertainty**: Regulatory approval processes could add years to any commercial deployment
- **International variation**: Different countries have different regulatory approaches, creating uncertainty

## 6. Integration Challenges (The "Last Mile")

- **UNITY-2 facility**: First non-nuclear integrated test of closed-loop fuel cycle, commissioning late 2026
- **Systems integration**: "The competitive moat has shifted from the plasma to the pipes"
- **Grid connection**: Fusion plants need to interface with existing grid infrastructure
- **Maintenance access**: Highly radioactive components in D-T reactors require remote maintenance

## Assessment: Which Barriers Could Be Overcome by 2030?

| Barrier | Solvable by 2030? | Confidence |
|---------|-------------------|------------|
| Scientific Q > 1 | Already done (NIF) | 100% |
| Scientific Q > 10 (tokamak) | Possible (SPARC) | 40-60% |
| Tritium breeding demonstrated | Unlikely at scale | 10-20% |
| Materials qualified for power plant | No | <5% |
| Net electricity from fusion | Extremely unlikely | <5% |
| Commercial fusion power | No | <1% |
