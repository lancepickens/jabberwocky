splatvid explainer

From a video to a 3D scene you can orbit

splatvid takes an ordinary video — you, walking around something — and turns it into a 3D gaussian splat model. Every stage is implemented from scratch: recovering the cameras with structure from motion, and painting the scene with hundreds of thousands of tiny translucent ellipsoids optimized by gradient descent. This page explains how each piece works.

The one idea underneath everything: parallax

A single photo destroys depth — every point along a ray lands on the same pixel. But a moving camera sees each scene point from different positions, and nearby points shift across the image faster than distant ones. That shift is parallax, and it is the only depth signal splatvid uses. Two views of the same point define two rays in space; where the rays cross is where the point is.

Consequence for shooting video: the camera must translate, not just rotate. A pan from a tripod produces zero parallax and cannot be reconstructed — the pipeline detects this (rotation-only pairs are fully explained by a homography) and refuses to initialize from such frames.

Stage 1 · video.pyPicking frames worth keeping

SfM cost grows quadratically with frame count, and motion blur poisons feature matching. So splatvid divides the timeline into ~60 windows and keeps the sharpest frame in each, scored by variance of the Laplacian: blur is a low-pass filter, the Laplacian responds only to high spatial frequencies, so sharp frames score high. Frames are downscaled to ≤ 960 px — plenty for sub-pixel feature localization at a fraction of the cost.

Stage 2 · features.py, sfm.py, ba.pyStructure from motion: where was the camera?

2.1 Find and match features

Each frame gets up to 4000 SIFT keypoints — scale- and rotation-invariant corners with a 128-number descriptor summarizing the local gradient pattern. Descriptors are matched between temporally close frames (a sliding window, plus sparse long-range pairs that close the loop when the camera comes back around). Raw matches lie, so three filters run in series:

  • Ratio test — a match must be clearly better than the runner-up (best distance < 0.75 × second best), killing ambiguous repetitive texture.
  • Mutual check — A→B must also be the best match B→A.
  • Epipolar check — all matches of a pair must be consistent with some rigid two-view geometry (a RANSAC-fit fundamental matrix). For a static scene, a correct match is constrained to a single line in the other image; anything off that line is a mismatch or a moving object.

Surviving matches are chained across frames into tracks — one track = one physical point seen in many frames.

2.2 Bootstrap from the best pair

One well-chosen pair starts the map: many verified matches and real parallax (pairs explained by a homography — pure rotation or a flat wall — are penalized). From that pair the essential matrix is estimated and decomposed into the relative rotation and translation direction; the first camera is pinned at the origin, and every inlier match is triangulated into a seed point cloud. Scale is arbitrary — a monocular video cannot tell a small scene nearby from a big one far away, and never needs to.

2.3 Grow the map: register, triangulate, repeat

2.4 Bundle adjustment: make it globally consistent

Greedy incremental steps accumulate drift, so every few registrations — and again at the end — bundle adjustment re-optimizes everything at once: all camera poses, all 3D points, and the shared focal length, minimizing total squared reprojection error (predicted pixel minus observed pixel) under a robust loss that shrugs off remaining bad matches.

minf, Rc, tc, Xp  Σ(c,p) ρ( ‖ π(f, Rc, tc, Xp) − uvc,p ‖² )

The trick that makes this tractable is sparsity: each residual touches only its own camera (6 numbers), its own point (3 numbers), and the focal length. The solver is told this pattern explicitly, so the huge Jacobian is estimated in a handful of evaluations instead of one per parameter.

Output of stage 2: calibrated camera poses for (nearly) every frame and a sparse, colored point cloud with sub-pixel average reprojection error. On splatvid's synthetic test — a rendered orbit where ground truth is known — SfM registers 40/40 cameras at 0.19 px mean error and recovers the focal length within 0.1 %.

Stage 3 · model.py, render.py, train.pyGaussian splatting: painting the scene with fuzzy ellipsoids

The sparse cloud knows where surfaces are, not what they look like. Stage 3 turns each point into a 3D gaussian — a soft ellipsoid with position μ, covariance Σ (shape/orientation/size), an RGB color, and an opacity — and then optimizes all of them so renders match the real frames.

3.1 Why gaussians specifically

  • Under perspective projection a 3D gaussian maps (to first order) to a 2D gaussian on screen — rendering needs no ray marching, just ellipse evaluation and blending.
  • Every parameter affects the image smoothly, so gradient descent works.
  • Capacity is adaptive — it is just a set; add gaussians where detail is missing, delete useless ones.

The covariance is built as Σ = R S Sᵀ Rᵀ from a unit quaternion R and per-axis scales S — always a valid ellipsoid, no constraints to enforce.

3.2 Compositing: how splats become an image

Per pixel, splats are blended front-to-back. Each contributes its color weighted by its own alpha and by the transmittance — how much light the splats in front have let through:

αi = oi · exp( −½ (x−ui)ᵀ Σ₂D⁻¹ (x−ui) )      C(x) = Σi ci αi Ti,    Ti = Πj<i (1 − αj)
Try it — front-to-back alpha compositing.

Three splats sit on one line of sight, violet in front, then teal, then amber, over a dark background. Drag the opacities and watch each splat's actual contribution c·α·T — and how little is left for whatever sits behind.

Bars show values at the shared center of the three splats. During training, autograd differentiates straight through this blend — every pixel's error pushes on the colors, opacities, shapes and positions of every splat that touched it.

3.3 Making it fast enough: tiles

Evaluating every gaussian at every pixel would be O(N·pixels). But a splat only touches pixels inside its 3σ ellipse, so the screen is cut into 16×16-pixel tiles; each gaussian is binned into the tiles its bounding box overlaps, and each tile blends only its own (depth-sorted) list as one vectorized operation. This same tiling is how the reference CUDA implementation works — splatvid's version is pure PyTorch: slower, but readable, portable, and differentiable by autograd with zero custom kernels.

3.4 Training and densification

Each iteration renders one random training view and descends the gradient of 0.8·L1 + 0.2·(1−SSIM) — L1 anchors color, SSIM protects local structure (edges, texture) that plain averaging would blur. Adam runs with per-parameter learning rates; position steps scale with scene size and decay over training.

The initial cloud is too sparse for fine detail, so every 150 iterations the model reshapes itself using an error signal the optimizer already computes: the accumulated screen-space gradient on each splat's position. A splat the loss keeps shoving around is trying to explain more than it can:

  • Split big restless gaussians into two smaller children — adds geometric resolution.
  • Clone small restless gaussians in place — adds coverage.
  • Prune splats whose opacity fell below 0.005 (the optimizer's way of saying “useless”) and huge fog-like floaters.

Stage 4 · export.py, viewer.htmlExport and the web viewer

Two files are written. scene.ply is the interchange format used by the reference implementation and splat editors — it stores raw optimization values (log-scales, opacity logits, SH color coefficients). scene.splat is the compact web format: 32 bytes per gaussian, positions and scales as float32, color and rotation quantized to bytes:

bytestypecontent
0–113 × float32position x, y, z
12–233 × float32scale x, y, z
24–274 × uint8R, G, B, A
28–314 × uint8quaternion (w,x,y,z), q·128+128

The bundled viewer is one dependency-free WebGL2 page. It re-derives the same EWA projection in the vertex shader, stretching one instanced quad per splat to its 2σ screen ellipse; the fragment shader evaluates the gaussian falloff. Correct blending needs depth order, so the CPU counting-sorts all splats along the view axis (O(N), 65 536 buckets) and redraws back-to-front with premultiplied alpha — re-sorting only when the view direction has actually rotated. That is the entire trick behind smooth splat rendering in a browser.

Does it actually work? Verifying without ground truth

Real videos come with no ground truth, so splatvid tests itself on a loop: it renders a synthetic orbit video of a procedurally generated scene using its own rasterizer, then feeds those pixels through the full pipeline as if they were real footage. The recovered cameras must form the known ring (40/40 registered, 0.19 px reprojection error, focal length within 0.1 %), and the trained model must reproduce the frames (≈18.5 dB PSNR after a deliberately short CPU schedule). Unit tests separately pin down the geometry math, the rasterizer's compositing order and gradient flow, and the export formats.

Limitations, honestly

  • Static scenes only. Moving objects violate the rigid-scene epipolar constraint and are filtered out as noise — or corrupt the pose solution.
  • The camera must translate. Pure panning has no parallax (by design, initialization refuses it).
  • Pinhole model: one shared focal length, no lens distortion — fisheye or rolling-shutter footage degrades accuracy.
  • Flat per-splat color (SH degree 0): shiny, view-dependent surfaces get averaged.
  • Pure-PyTorch speed: exact but slow on CPU — defaults train at small resolution; on a GPU the same code takes much higher budgets.
